The Brief

Revenue was growing. The questions underneath it had no answers.

The client had two years of sales history plus a current-year slice and a stock export, spread across spreadsheets with duplicated product codes, inconsistent units, and mislabeled categories. Leadership could see a topline number, but couldn't answer the questions that actually drive a distribution business: Which customers are we losing? How concentrated is our revenue? What stock is dead money on a shelf?

My job: make the data trustworthy, then build the model and dashboard that answer those questions at a glance.

Approach

Data cleaning and modeling came before any chart.

A dashboard is only as reliable as the work done before it's built. Visualizing data that hasn't been cleaned and properly modeled produces a report that looks finished but can't be trusted.

Clean

Made the raw data trustworthy

The exports carried real quality issues that quietly distort every total.

  • Deduplicated & normalized product codes (variant suffixes collapsed to one SKU)
  • Standardized packaging units to a common per-piece basis
  • Standardized category casing and brand-as-category mislabels; one residual split (drapes/Drapes) was caught later and flagged in the findings report
  • Resolved Not Found regions and mapped sales officers
Model

Built a star-schema semantic model

A fact table of transactions surrounded by clean dimensions: the foundation for fast, consistent numbers.

  • Product, Customer, Date & Stock dimensions with proper relationships
  • 50+ DAX measures: YoY, time-intelligence, churn/retention, ABC, inventory risk
  • Reusable measure layer organized into display folders
Visualize

Designed a five-page executive report

Every page answers one business question and leads with the summary before the detail.

  • Consistent KPI band, slicers & drill paths across pages
  • Charts chosen by the job: trend, ranking, proportion, risk
  • Clean, accessible theme; labels and titles written for humans
What the data revealed

Four findings that changed the conversation.

Finding 01 · Growth

Revenue grew 6.5% — but on a shrinking customer base

Topline revenue rose from $3.59M to $3.83M year-over-year. Healthy on its own, until you notice the active customer count actually fell from 324 to 303. Growth came from existing large accounts spending more, not from a widening base.

Finding 02 · Churn

37% of customers churned in a single year

Of 324 customers active in 2024, 121 stopped buying entirely in 2025. New wins (100) nearly replaced them in count, masking the loss in the topline, but a 37% churn rate is a retention problem hiding behind a growth number.

Finding 03 · Concentration

The top 10 customers drive 57% of revenue

Revenue is dangerously concentrated: just 10 accounts out of 439 account for 56.9% of sales. The flip side of "big accounts are growing" is fragility: losing one or two of them would swamp any gains from the long tail.

Finding 04 · The real risk

The expensive losses came from customers who never left

Those 121 churned customers cost just $61K, about 1.7% of prior-year revenue, because they were small accounts. The expensive problem was a different cohort: 90 customers who never left but quietly cut their spending, which cost $759K (twelve times the churn number). A win-back campaign aimed at the 121 would have chased the wrong target.

Recommendations

From dashboard to decisions.

A dashboard is only worth building if it changes what someone does on Monday. These followed directly from the findings.

Protect the top 10 accounts

With 57% of revenue in 10 accounts, formalize key-account management and early-warning alerts on any spending dip: the dashboard's YoY and status flags make this monitorable.

Split churn from decline

Automate win-back for the 121 small churned accounts. Route the 90 quiet decliners (worth 12x more) to direct account management instead; they're still reachable.

Rebalance at-risk inventory

The inventory page flags overstock (excess Days-of-Cover) and dead stock. Redeploy or discount these before they become write-offs.

Focus on the core categories

Sutures and Drapes are ~69% of revenue between them. ABC analysis on the SKU table shows exactly which items earn their shelf space and which to prune.
